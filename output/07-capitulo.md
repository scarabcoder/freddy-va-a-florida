# Capítulo VII

Una tarde[^tarde], while los animales were marching southward, they came to a deep, dark bosque de pinos[^pinos]. Hacía calor, for they were getting near Florida now, and the camino was very rough and stony. They were all hot and tired and cross. Even the good-natured Mrs. Wiggins grumbled as they plodded along between the rows of tall, gloomy pinos.

"I wish these woods would come to an end," dijo she. "I never saw such a place! Nothing but pine needles—no zacate, no agua. And it's almost supper-time, too."

Robert put his nariz up in the air and sniffed. "I smell lluvia," dijo. And just as he dijo it, there came a long, low rumble of distant truenos[^truenos].

"Well, we have got to find a shed or a granero or something," dijo Hank, the old, white caballo. "I'm not going to stand under a tree in a tormenta[^tormenta] for anybody."

"¡Dios mío!" dijo Henrietta crossly. "What's the good of _talking_! Why don't you _do_ something? Jinx, why don't you climb a tree and see if you can see a granero?"

This was sensible advice, so Jinx climbed up to the top of the tallest pino he could find. When he came down, dijo: "I saw the sol going down in the west, and I saw a tormenta coming up in the south. And the woods go on for millas and millas. But about half a milla farther along there is a little cabaña[^cabana] de troncos. And there is a chimenea[^chimenea] on the house, and there is humo[^humo] going into the chimenea."

"Coming out of the chimenea, you mean," dijo Hank.

"I mean just what I say," dijo Jinx. "There is humo coming from all parts of the cielo and gathering into a cloud and pouring down the chimenea."

"¡Tonterías![^tonterias]" exclamó Henrietta. "I never heard of such nonsense!"

"I don't know whether it's nonsense or not," dijo the gato, "but that's what I saw. If you're so smart, why don't you climb up the tree and take a look yourself?"

Henrietta didn't dare climb the tree, so she dijo "¡Tonterías!" again in a very loud voz, and walked off.

It was getting darker and darker, and the truenos were rumbling and rolling and coming nearer and nearer.

"Well, there's no use quarrelling about it," dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "If there's a house, there's a granero, and if there's a granero, maybe we can get into it, out of the lluvia. I'm going along." And as this was a very sensible speech, they all started along after her.

Pretty soon they saw the cabaña de troncos. It sat back from the camino, and was almost hidden by the trees and bushes that grew close up to its paredes[^paredes], so that if Jinx had not seen it from the tree-top, they might have walked right by and never noticed it. And sure enough, round the top of the chimenea was whirling what looked like a cloud of humo. It whirled round and round, and then plunged down, and as the animales had never seen humo going the wrong way before, they just stood and stared at it.

"What did I tell you?" dijo Jinx.

But Henrietta didn't answer him; she went up close to the house and looked and looked; and although it was getting pretty dark, she saw that it wasn't humo after all, but a parvada of pájaros, who were coming from every direction and dropping down the chimenea.

"There's your humo!" exclamó scornfully. "Chimney-swallows! They live in the chimenea, and they're going home to sleep. ¡Humo, indeed! That's the cat of it! Jumping at conclusions!"

"I'll jump at you if you say any more," dijo Jinx, "and pull all your tail plumas out."

"Come, come," dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "Stop your fighting, animales. If there are golondrinas in that chimenea, it means that there hasn't been a fire built in the house in a long time. And that means that nobody lives there. Let's get inside."

¡BOOM, BOOM, BUM-BOOM! went the truenos. And the animales made a rush for the puerta and got inside just as the lluvia came down with a swish and a rattle.

There was only one room in the house, and in it were two sillas[^sillas] and a mesa and an empty barrel and a pile of old periódicos[^periodicos]. Opposite the puerta was a big chimenea, and beside the chimenea was a neat pile of leña[^lena]. But everything was very dusty. Nobody had lived in the house in a long time.

Outside, the lluvia was coming down in torrents, and the truenos and relámpagos[^relampagos] were very bad indeed. But the animales were happy because they were dry. Only the ratones, Eek and Quik and Eeny and Cousin Augustus, were rather frightened, and at the first really sharp relámpago they dove down an old mouse hole by the chimenea and didn't come up until the tormenta was over.

After the truenos and relámpagos had gone farther away again, and the lluvia had settled down to a good, steady, all-night pour, Robert dijo: "It's getting cold. I wish Mr. Bean was here to build us a fuego[^fuego]."

"There are some cerillos[^cerillos] up here," dijo Charles, the gallo, who had perched on the mantel over the chimenea.

"I believe I could build one myself," dijo Robert. "I've seen him do it often enough. Chuck down a couple of cerillos, Charles."

"And what about all those golondrinas in the chimenea?" preguntó Henrietta. "I suppose you never thought about them!"

"We'll invite 'em to come down and sit around the fuego with us," dijo Robert. He called up to the golondrinas and invited them down, and pretty soon they began dropping down in twos and threes. They circled round the room, and then took their places in rows along the paredes, for golondrinas don't perch as other pájaros do, holding on by their garras—they hang themselves up by the little hooks they have on the tops of their alas. There were so many of them that the log paredes were covered with them, and they looked like a beautiful, shining negro tapestry.

Then Robert built the fuego with periódicos and leña, and he held a cerillo between his teeth and scratched it on the suelo[^suelo] and dropped it on the periódicos. He singed his nariz before he got through, but at last he got the periódicos to burning. Then all the animales had to squat down on the hearth and blow the fuego to make it go, because he hadn't built it very well. But at last it burned up brightly, and then they all sat round and talked.

"I'd like to know who lived in this house," dijo Charles.

"Nobody knows," dijo the oldest golondrina, who was hanging just over the puerta. And all the other golondrinas dijo: "¡Así es![^asies]" and rustled their alas.

"Nobody lived here in my grandfather's time," dijo the oldest golondrina.

"¡Así es!" dijo the other golondrinas.

"And nobody lived here in my great-grandfather's time."

"¡Así es!"

"And nobody lived here in my great-great-grandfather's time."

"¡Así es!"

"And nobody——"

"Excuse me," dijo Robert politely, "but I don't think you need go any farther back. Don't people ever come here at all?"

"Once in a while—" the golondrina began slowly. Before he could go on, the youngest golondrina piped up: "¡Así es!"

The oldest golondrina glared at him crossly, and his mother spanked him soundly for speaking out of turn. For it is a custom among the golondrinas for the oldest and wisest one to do all the talking, and for the others to say "¡Así es!" when he has finished. They do this because there are so many of them, and if they all talked at once in their little twittery voices, nobody would be able to understand what they were talking about.

"Once in a while," the oldest golondrina went on, when the little golondrina had been spanked and sent off to cry softly in a rincón, "men come out to this house to look for the dinero that is supposed to be hidden here. It is said that a bag of twenty-dollar gold pieces is concealed in or somewhere near the house. But if that is so, nobody has ever found the tesoro[^tesoro]."

The animales were all very much excited at this piece of news. Of course they could not use dinero themselves, but they thought how nice it would be if they could find the oro and take it back to Mr. Bean, who needed dinero so badly. Then he could buy all the things he wanted, and could repair the granero and the gallinero, and perhaps put stoves in them to keep the animales warm in cold weather. So they all started hunting for the place where the dinero was hidden.

Hank tapped with his hoof on the suelo and the paredes, to see if they sounded hollow, and Jinx, the gato, climbed up on all the shelves and peered into cupboards, and Eek and Quik and Eeny and Cousin Augustus went back down the old mouse hole in the rincón and scurried round under the suelo and explored every crack and crevice. Even el señor and la señora Webb slipped into the many cracks in the paredes and chimenea and looked round. But they didn't find any sign of the dinero.

"The only place we haven't looked is the chimenea," dijo Freddy, the puerco, at last.

"It is not in the chimenea," dijo the oldest golondrina. And all the other golondrinas dijo: "¡Así es!"

"Well, I guess we'll have to give it up," dijo Freddy. "It isn't in the house, and if it's buried outside, we could never possibly find it."

And so they gave it up and came back and sat round the fuego and told cuentos and played guessing games till bedtime.
