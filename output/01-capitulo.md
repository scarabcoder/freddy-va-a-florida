# Capítulo I

Charles, el gallo[^gallo], came out of the front door of the gallinero[^gallinero] and walked slowly across the corral[^corral]. It was still muy oscuro[^oscuro] in the corral, for it was half past four de la mañana[^manana], and the sol[^sol] was not yet up. He shivered and thought of his nice warm perch in the coop, but había una razón[^razon] why he did not go back to it. Mr. Bean, el granjero[^granjero], did not have very much dinero[^dinero], and could not afford to buy a despertador[^despertador], and he relied on the gallo to wake him up bright and early in the morning. The last time Charles had overslept, Mr. Bean had been very enojado[^enojado] and had threatened to have him fricasseed with baking-powder biscuit for the comida del domingo[^domingo]. Charles did not like getting up early, before any of the other pájaros[^pajaros] and animales[^animales] were stirring, but he felt that it was better to get up than to be fricasseed. And so this morning he hopped sleepily on to a poste[^poste] and, after clearing his garganta[^garganta] several times, began to crow: “Cock-a-doodle-doo! Cock-a-doodle-doo-oo-oo!!”

The eastern cielo[^cielo] grew brighter and brighter and more rosado[^rosado] and more rosado, and for a long time nothing else happened. Then some petirrojos[^petirrojos] began talking together in little voces bajitas[^voces] in the big olmo[^olmo] that grew by the granero[^granero], and a young ardilla[^ardilla] came scampering along the cerca[^cerca] and stopped on the poste next to Charles and started to wash his cara[^cara] with his paws, and down in the house where the puercos[^puercos] lived there was a great grunting and squealing, so that Charles knew that the puercos were beginning to think about el desayuno[^desayuno]. And he crowed some more.

And at last, far off across the campos[^campos], where the cielo came down to meet them, there appeared a little spark of bright oro[^oro], that grew and grew until it looked like a fogata[^fogata], and then like a house on fire, and then like a whole ciudad[^ciudad] burning up. And that was the upper edge of the sol, coming up from the other side of the mundo[^mundo], where toda la noche[^noche] it had been shining on Chinese pagodas and the Himalayas and jungles in Africa and all the queer places where la gente[^gente] work and play while we are sound asleep.

And just as the edge of the sol came into sight, the head of Mr. Bean, el granjero, appeared at his bedroom ventana[^ventana]. He had on a white cotton gorro de dormir[^gorro] with a red tassel, and his cara was completely hidden behind his bushy, barbas grises[^barbas], so that nobody, not even his esposa[^esposa], had ever seen what he really looked like. And he was looking out to see what kind of a day it was going to be.

As soon as Mr. Bean poked his head out of the ventana, Charles hopped down from the poste. His day’s trabajo[^trabajo] was already done, but although he was still cold and sleepy, he did not go back to the gallinero. For his esposa and her ocho hermanas[^hermanas] were up by that time. “And nobody could get a minute’s peace in all that cackle,” he muttered angrily. “I’ll go take forty winks in the granero.”

“Buenos días[^buenosdias], Charles,” dijo[^dijo] Hank, the old, white caballo[^caballo], whose stall was nearest the puerta[^puerta]. “Touch of invierno[^invierno] in the air this morning.”

Charles flew up and perched on the edge of Hank’s pesebre[^pesebre].

“Touch of invierno!” he exclamó[^exclamo]. “I guess there is! It’s frío[^frio], that’s what it is—downright frío!”

“Well, we’ve got to expect it now,” dijo Hank. “Nieve[^nieve] will be flying in another month or two.”

“Ugh!” dijo Charles, and shivered.

“There’s less work for me in invierno,” dijo Hank; “though I must say I prefer the verano[^verano]. I’ve got a touch of reumatismo[^reumatismo] in my off hind pata[^pata], and these cold nights set it aching.”

“Of course,” dijo Charles sympathetically. “They would! It’s a shame. You ought to have a cobija[^cobija] or something to cover you, and this granero is a terribly draughty old place. But Mr. Bean, he never thinks how the animales and pájaros suffer; he sleeps warm under his feather-bed and four patchwork colchas[^colchas]—_he_ doesn’t care as long as _he’s_ warm! Now, take me. Every morning, invierno and verano, I have to get up before daylight, crawl out of my comfortable gallinero, and crow and get things started on the granja[^granja], just because he’s too stingy to buy himself a despertador. Doesn’t matter how cold and rainy it is, it has to be done. And if I miss a morning, what do I get? I get fricasseed, that’s what!”

“It seems sort of hard,” dijo Hank.

“I guess it does! And now invierno’s coming. I detest invierno! But I’ve got to get out, just the same, and wade around in the nieve and freeze my bill. I wouldn’t mind so much if I was warm the rest of the time. If there was a stove in the gallinero, and a couple of good wool cobijas to sleep under. That gallinero ought to have a cellar under it, too—the floor’s as frío as stone.”

Hank sighed. “Yes,” he asintió[^asintio], “it’s a hard life. There’s no denying it. But what can we do about it?”

A little twittering voice respondió[^respondio] from high up under the techo[^techo]. “Why don’t you migrar[^migrar]?” it dijo.

They looked up, but it was oscuro under the techo, and they could not see anything.

“Who are you?” preguntó[^pregunto] Charles. “And what are you talking about?”

“I’m a golondrina[^golondrina],” dijo the voice, “and I’m talking about migrar. We pájaros all migrate every year, and I don’t see why you can’t do it too, if you don’t like the invierno.”

“Oh, you don’t!” dijo Charles rather crossly. “Well, suppose you tell us what you’re talking about. If it’s worth listening to. We can’t keep track of everything you little unimportant pájaros do.”

Charles, being a farm bird, felt very superior to all the wild pájaros, and he puffed out his chest with importance. But the golondrina only laughed chirpingly.

“You needn’t be so grand,” she dijo. “After all, you’ve never been outside your own corral, and you have to do as you’re told, or you get fricasseed. And I’ve travelled thousands of millas[^millas] in my time, and I don’t take orders from anyone.”

“Well, of all the—!” Charles began angrily. But Hank shook his head at him.

“She may have something interesting to tell us,” he dijo. And then he preguntó the golondrina politely if she would explain to them what migrar was.

So she told them that every fall, when it began to get cold, the pájaros gathered together in big parvadas[^parvadas] and started south. They travelled hundreds of millas, and some of them went to Florida, and some went to Central or South America. All winter long, she dijo, it was soleado[^soleado] and cálido[^calido] down sur[^sur]. There was never any nieve, never any cold winds, and there was always plenty to eat. And then in the primavera[^primavera] they came back north again.

When she had told them this, she dropped with a twitter from the techo and shot like an arrow out through the open puerta into the warm sunshine.

“Do you believe it?” Charles preguntó when she had gone. He felt it beneath his dignidad[^dignidad] to pay much attention to anything a golondrina could tell him, although he was really very much interested.

“Yes,” dijo Hank. “I have heard of it before. And it sounds pretty good. But it wouldn’t do for me, I’m afraid. It’s a long road to Florida. If I could volar[^volar], though, I won’t say that I wouldn’t try it.”

“I can’t volar,” dijo Charles. “Not much, that is. But I would walk a good many miles to find a lugar[^lugar] where it is cálido and soleado all winter, and where I shouldn’t have to get up in the morning till I got good and ready. It wouldn’t be any fun going alone, of course, but if we could get up a party——”

“If you could get up a party,” dijo Hank, “I won’t say I shouldn’t like to go myself.”

Charles jumped down from the pesebre. “I’m going to see some of the other animales,” he dijo. “If they’re interested, we’ll have a junta[^junta] to-night and talk it over.” And he went out into the corral.

The more he thought about it, the more excited he became. He went out into the huerto[^huerto] and talked to an oriole and a couple of blackbirds, and the tales they told him of the lazy life they led in the tropical, southern sunshine fairly made his mouth water. Then he went to see the puercos and the vacas[^vacas] and the other animales, and they were very much interested and dijo that they had all been dreading the long, cold invierno, and that if he really knew of a lugar where it was cálido and soleado they would be very glad to go there. So he invited them to come to a junta that evening in the establo[^establo], where he would tell them all about it, and those who wanted to go could talk it over and decide when to start.
