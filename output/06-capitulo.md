# Capítulo VI

Por dos semanas[^semanas] the animales continued traveling toward Florida.

"It must be a long way," dijo Hank. "The weather doesn't seem to get any warmer."

"But it doesn't get any colder, either," dijo Mrs. Wiggins, "and down here the hojas[^hojas] are still on the trees. When we left home, the trees round the granja had all shed their hojas and were ready for the invierno."

"Well, I don't care how far it is," dijo Hank. "We're certainly having a good time. I shall be almost sorry when we get there."

Nearly every día now large parvadas of pájaros passed by overhead, southward bound. And one morning the same golondrina who had first put the idea of migrar into Charles's head dropped down from the cielo and circled about over them. She had left home two días earlier, and she gave them all the news of the granja, and messages from their relatives, and told them that Mr. and Mrs. Bean were well, but that they felt very bad that the animales had left them.

"At first," dijo the golondrina, "Mr. Bean thought someone had stolen you, but then somehow he guessed that you had decided to go to Florida for the invierno. I heard him tell Mrs. Bean that he hoped you'd have a good time and come back safe and sound in the primavera. And he dijo that he was going to try to make things more comfortable for you, although he didn't know how he'd manage it, because he didn't have enough dinero to fix things up the way they ought to be."

When the animales heard this, they felt a little sorry that they had left Mr. Bean without saying good-bye. "But we'll bring him something nice from Florida when we go back," they dijo.

So far they had kept away from las ciudades as much as possible, because they were afraid that la gente would not understand that they were migrating, and would try to lock them up and keep them. And when they had to go through aldeas, they always waited till late at noche, when everyone was asleep. But at last one día, far off in the distance, they saw a little speck of oro that glittered and sparkled in the bright sunlight.

They wondered and wondered what the oro thing could be, but none of them knew, and pretty soon, as they went along, the camino turned into a calle[^calle], and there were houses on both sides of it and trolley tracks down the middle. And the speck of oro grew bigger and bigger. It looked as if a great golden balloon was tethered among the trees ahead of them.

"We're coming to a ciudad," dijo Robert. "We'd better turn off this camino and go round it."

"I wish I knew what the oro thing is," dijo Freddy. Freddy had a very inquiring mind.

Just then a little pequeño[^pequeno] perro, very woolly and white, with a fancy listón[^liston] azul[^azul] around his neck came along, and Freddy preguntó him.

The pequeño perro stuck his nariz up in the air. "Don't speak to me, you common puerco," dijo.

"Eh?" dijo Freddy. "What's the matter with you? I only asked you a civil question."

"Go away, you vulgar creature," dijo the pequeño perro snippily.

"Oho!" dijo Freddy. "You're too stuck up to talk to a puerco, are you?" And he laughed and ran at the pequeño perro and rolled him over and over in the camino till his white coat and listón azul were both grey with polvo. Then he stood him on his patas and dijo: "Now answer my question."

Then the pequeño perro meekly told him that the thing that looked like a golden balloon was the cúpula[^cupula] of the Capitol, and that the ciudad they were coming to was Washington, where the President lives. And when Freddy had given him a lecture on politeness and helped him to brush the polvo off himself, he let him go.

"I'd like to see the President," dijo Hank.

All the others dijo they would too, but they were afraid to go into the ciudad because la gente might lock them up, and boys were sure to throw piedras at them.

But Jinx, the gato, dijo: "I vote we go, just the same. I don't believe the President will let them do anything to us. And we can see the Capitol and maybe go up to the White House and call on the President."

So they decided to go, and started down the calle toward the ciudad. All la gente came out on their door-steps to watch them go by, but nobody bothered them, and by and by they came to the Capitol. They stood for a long time and admired the big, white edificio[^edificio], with its many columns and its gilded cúpula, and then they walked round to the side and admired it some more, and while they were standing there, two senadores[^senadores] in sombreros de copa[^sombreros] came out and saw them.

"I didn't know animales ever visited the Capitol," dijo the first senador.

"Neither did I," dijo the second senador. "But I don't see why they shouldn't. I think it's rather nice."

Then a third senador came out and joined the other two, and he dijo: "By George! I have heard about these animales! They belong to one of my constituents. They're going to Florida for the invierno, and I believe they're the first animales that ever migrated. This, gentlemen, is one of the most important occurrences in the annals of this august assemblage. I'm going to order a banda[^banda], and take them round and show them the ciudad."

So he went in and ordered the banda, and told the other senadores, who put their heads out of the windows and smiled and waved to the animales.

"What's a constituent?" preguntó Mrs. Wiggins.

But none of the others could tell her, and to this day she has never found out.

Pretty soon the banda came, and they struck up "Marching Through Georgia," and went up the wide avenida[^avenida] toward the White House, and the animales marched behind. First came the senador in his sombrero de copa, and then Charles and Henrietta, and then Mrs. Wiggins, with the ratones sitting on her back, and then the two perros and Freddy, the puerco, and then Hank, with Alice and Emma on _his_ back, and last came Jinx. They all walked in time to the music and held their cabezas up and pretended not to see any of la gente that crowded the sidewalks, as everyone always does when he is in a desfile[^desfile]. Beside them walked twenty policías[^policias], to keep la gente back and to prevent them from pulling the tail plumas out of los patos or las gallinas to keep as souvenirs.

They went all over the ciudad, and the senador showed them all the fine edificios and parks and monuments, and last they came to the White House. And there was the President out on the front porch, smiling and bowing to them, and as they filed past, he shook them each by a garra or a pata or a hoof. Even Eek and Quik and Eeny and Cousin Augustus overcame their timidity and put their tiny patas into the President's big hand. They were all very proud.

[Illustration: THEY ALL WALKED IN TIME TO THE MUSIC.]

And then they went on with the banda playing a different tune every ten minutes, and la gente cheering and waving handkerchiefs. When they got to the edge of the ciudad, the banda stopped and the senador made them a discurso, which began:

"Friends and constituents, I am very sensible of the honour which you have done me to-day. To welcome a delegation of the home folks to the Nation's Capital is one of the few pleasures that cheer the burdened brow of those whose stern duty it is to keep their shoulder always to the wheel of the ship of State. And that reminds me of the story of the two Irishmen."

He told the story, and the animales laughed politely, although they did not see anything very funny about it, and that is why it is not written down here. Nor is the rest of the senador's discurso written down, for the animales did not understand much of it, and I am not at all sure that the senador did either. But all agreed that it was a stirring discurso.

Then the senador said good-bye to the adventurers, and the banda played "Auld Lang Syne," and the animales went on their way.

"Well," dijo Mrs. Wiggins con un suspiro[^suspiro], as she dropped off to sleep that noche, "we certainly had a grand time. But I do wish I knew what a constituent is."
