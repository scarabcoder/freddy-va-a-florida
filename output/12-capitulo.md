# Capítulo XII

Durante los siguientes dos meses los animales visitaron todos los lugares principales de interés en Florida, and saw all there was to see. Visitaron Palm Beach, los Everglades, Miami y el Gran Pantano de Cipreses. And it was on the way across a corner of the pantano[^pantano] that they had a very exciting aventura.

Así fue como sucedió. Cuando llegaron por primera vez al pantano, most of the animales were afraid and did not want to go into it at all, for it stretched for millas and millas, and there were no caminos or paths, and there was no firm ground to walk on, only agua and lodo[^lodo] and the great twisted, gnarly cypress roots. Estaba oscuro, también, because the trees grew so thick.

Pero Jinx dijo: "¡Ándale! Let's see what it's like. We don't have to go very far in. ¿De qué tienen miedo?"

Y así entraron.

Al principio no era muy difícil caminar, but soon the lodo and agua got deeper and the trees thicker. Y después de un rato there wasn't anything to walk on at all—only agua and trees.

"Me regreso," dijo Mrs. Wiggins. Y los otros animales dijo que ellos también. Even Jinx agreed they couldn't go any farther.

Pero cuando quisieron regresar, they found that they hadn't the slightest idea which way to go. Se habían girado y torcido among the trees so many times that they didn't know from which direction they had come. The agua covered their footprints so they couldn't follow them. Y sobre sus cabezas the branches were so thick que no podían ver el sol.

"¡Ahora sí estamos metidos en un lío!" dijo Henrietta, who had been riding on Hank's back. "¡Espero que estés satisfecho, Jinx!"

"No ayuda nada ponerse a insultar," dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "Come along, let's try this direction. One way is as good as another, and this looks as if it might be right."

Y así continuaron, with Mrs. Wiggins in the lead. Estaba muy oscuro y lúgubre. The agua was black, and long beards of grey musgo[^musgo] hung down from the ramas of the trees. Una y otra vez tuvieron que nadar, and the animales who could not nadar climbed on the larger animales' backs.

Por fin parecía que estaban llegando a tierra firme[^tierra]. Ahead of them they could see sunlight through the troncos[^troncos] de los árboles, and they floundered and stumbled onward as fast as they could go. En unos minutos llegaron a la orilla of what seemed to be a small canal, and beyond the canal was a grassy meadow, verde and pleasant in the bright sol.

"Bueno, esto seguramente no es el camino por donde vinimos," dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "¡Pero vaya, ese zacate se ve rico! Creo que podríamos comer unos cuantos bocados, ¿verdad, Hank? Come along, animales, vamos a nadar hasta allá. At least there's something to stand on."

"¡Cuidado! Don't bump your noses on those troncos," dijo Jinx, señalando with one garra to what looked like a lot of tree trunks, lying half under agua in the middle of the canal.

Así que todos nadaron. But as they were climbing out on the farther bank, Henrietta comenzó a cacarear excitedly. "¡Miren! ¡Miren! ¡Los troncos están cobrando vida!"

Y era verdad—what they had thought were troncos had suddenly started swimming after them. ¡Eran caimanes!

"¡Este lugar definitivamente no me gusta!" dijo Mrs. Wiggins. But like most vacas, she had a stout heart, and she turned round and lowered her cuernos and shook them threateningly at the caimanes. "¡Manténganse alejados!" dijo. "¡No toleraremos tonterías!"

But the caimanes only laughed, and one of them dijo: "¡Ajá! ¿Qué no? Well, what did you come into our country for, then?"

"Somos animales pacíficos," dijo Mrs. Wiggins, "and all we ask is to be shown the shortest way out of your country. Estamos perdidos, and we shall be very much obliged to you if you will help us. But if you won't help us, we shall have to find our own way out."

Then all the caimanes laughed so hard that two of them choked, and their friends had to whack them on the backs with their tails. Y dijeron: "¿Saben dónde están? Están en una isla[^isla] en el corazón del país de los caimanes. No pueden escapar[^escapar]. Y esta noche los caimanes los tendremos de cena."

Los animales vieron entonces que truly estaban en un grave aprieto. "Esto es incluso peor que ser fricasé," dijo Charles.

Pero Freddy, the clever puerco, had an idea. Y aunque estaba muy asustado[^susto], he said to the caimanes: "Señores, cometerán un gravísimo error if you eat us. We are not ordinary animales. We are the first animales in the world who ever migrated. We have come from far in the norte; miles and miles we have travelled, to visit your beautiful country, and to take back word of its loveliness to our people. ¿Seguramente no serían tan inhospitalarios as to eat us for supper?"

"Habla muy bien," dijo one of the caimanes, "but I am sure he would taste even better. ¡Qué gordo y redondo está!"

Pero otro dijo: "Puede que tengas razón, puerco. Te llevaremos ante el Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes, and you may tell him what you have told us. And perhaps he will let you go. And perhaps he will eat you for supper just the same. But that is for him to decide."

Y así los guió across the isla to where the agua and the pantano began again on the other side. And he stood on the bank and called: "Oh, Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes, there be extranjeros[^extranjeros] here who would have speech with thee."

Nada pasó por un momento, and then there was a bubbling and a boiling of the agua, and a huge head, as big as a barrel, appeared, and after the head a body as long as Mrs. Wiggins and Hank and Jack and Robert and Freddy together. It was the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes, and he was so old that there was green musgo growing all over him.

Abrió un sabio y viejo ojo, and his deep grumbling voz said sleepily: "¿Qué quieren?"

"Don't want to be eaten for supper," dijo the other caimán.

"Pues cómanlos en el almuerzo," dijo the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes, and began to sink out of sight again.

But Freddy rushed down to the edge of the agua and shouted: "¡Oh, Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes! Somos extranjeros en su hermoso país and we have come miles and miles to visit you and tell you of our own land, of which you have never heard."

The Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes opened both eyes and stopped sinking.

"¿Por qué no dijeron eso desde el principio?" he asked. "That alters the case entirely. I hear very little news of the great world in this quiet spot. Por favor, cuéntenme de su hogar."

"¡Oh, Abuelo de Todos los—" Freddy comenzó.

But the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes stopped him. "It will be better," he said, "if you call me simply abuelo." Y cerró los ojos and sank till everything but his ears was under agua, and prepared to listen.

Entonces Freddy les contó about the life they had lived up norte on Mr. Bean's granja, and of how cold it was in invierno, and of their viaje to the sur. Every time he stopped for breath, the caimanes, who were sitting around him in a circle, would say: "¡Sí, sí, continúe!" And Freddy went on until he was tired, and then Jinx took up the story until _he_ was tired, and then Charles went on with it. Y para cuando Charles había terminado, and they had told everything they could think of, it was almost sunset.

Then the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes came up to the top of the agua again and opened his eyes and dijo: "I thank you for telling us of your wonderful country. Ha sido muy interesante. And now, as it is almost supper-time, continuaremos con el festín[^festín]. I am sure you will all taste very much better for the entertainment you have given us."

At this the animales were very much alarmed. "¡No nos van a decir que siempre quisieron comernos!" they cried.

"Pues, claro," dijo the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes. "Nothing was ever said about our _not_ eating you, was there?"

Esto enfureció mucho a los animales, and Jinx was so mad that he almost had a fit. "¿Nos está diciendo," he screamed, "that you've let us talk ourselves hoarse for nothing, you great big, muddy, long-nosed, leather-skinned hippopotamus, you? ¡Debería darle vergüenza! ¿Qué creen que van a pensar todos los animales del norte cuando se enteren? ¡Comiendo a los visitantes que vienen a hacerles una visita amistosa! ¡Qué buena opinión van a tener de Florida!"

"¡Dios mío, claro que sí!" exclamó Mrs. Wiggins. "And the President of the United States, too. ¡Él nos dio la mano y nos deseó buen viaje! ¿Qué va a decir _él_?"

"¡Mandará a su ejército aquí y los arrojará a todos al océano!" dijo Jinx. "¡Eso es lo que hará!"

The Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes smiled, and his smile was eight feet broad. "Lo que dicen puede ser verdad," he remarked. "_Pero_—¿quién se lo va a decir? Answer me that. ¿Quién se lo va a decir? You, madam?" he asked Mrs. Wiggins. "No-o-o, creo que no. You'll be eaten up, cuernos, cascos y cola. And so——"

But Henrietta interrupted. "_Nosotros_ se lo diremos," she said. "My husband and I. You may eat the animales, but you can't eat us, because you can't catch us. _Nosotros_ podemos volar."

"My dear," dijo the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes, "tengo más de ochocientos años. I was centuries old when Ponce de León came to Florida to look for the Fountain of Youth. I remember Balboa well—a tall man with a black beard and a shiny steel hat. He made the same mistake you did, my friends—he mistook me for a tronco. But he was more fortunate than you. He got away with merely the loss of one of his boots." The Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes smiled at the memory. "A delicious boot that was, too—old Spanish leather. I chewed on it for half a día.

"Sí, como decía, soy muy viejo. Yet in all my eight hundred years I have never seen or heard of a gallina or a gallo who could fly like other pájaros."

Now it is true that gallinas and gallos cannot fly as well as most pájaros, but they don't like to be reminded of it. Henrietta se puso furiosa.

"¡Vaya si es así!" exclamó. "Well, if you've kept your eyes shut for eight hundred years, it's no wonder you don't know anything! ¿Nunca vio un gallo que pudiera volar, eh? Well, you're going to see one now. Charles," she dijo to her husband, "vuela hasta esos árboles al otro lado del agua."

Now the trees were quite a long way off, and Charles had never in his life flown farther than from the ground to the top of a cerca. "¡Dios mío, Henrietta!" he whispered, "I can't fly up there. I won't be able to go half that distance, and I'll fall into the agua and the caimanes will eat me."

"Te van a comer de todas formas si _no_ vuelas hasta allá," she whispered back. "Tienes que hacerlo. Es nuestra única oportunidad de escapar. If they think you will go back and tell the President, they will let us go."

"Bueno, lo intentaré," dijo Charles. So he kissed Henrietta good-bye and squared his hombros and flapped his alas and started, while all the animales cheered, and the caimanes giggled and poked each other in the ribs with their elbows.

Charles voló hacia arriba—up, up, higher than he had ever been before, as high as the tops of the trees. Y entonces empezó a cruzar el agua.

Down below, the animales held their breath as they watched him. They saw him flapping his alas so hard that plumas flew out of them and floated downward. But he could not get any higher; iba bajando lentamente hacia el agua, and two of the caimanes plunged in and swam out to be under him when he came down.

"Nunca lo va a lograr," dijo Mrs. Wiggins sadly. "¡Jamás de los jamases!"

But suddenly they saw him stop moving his alas. Extendió las alas and held them motionless, and then, to the amazement of all the onlookers, went straight across the agua—faster, faster, and landed with a flutter in the trees.

Lo que había pasado era esto. There was a strong wind blowing across the pantano, but the isla, shut in by walls of high trees, was like a room, and the wind did not come down there at all. It was this wind that had caught Charles and blown him safely across, but of course none of the onlookers knew this, and they thought that he had done it himself.

Then all the animales set up a great cheer, and the caimanes had nothing to say at all, and the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes opened his eyes wider than he had opened them in six hundred years and exclamó: "¡Bueno, por mi vida! ¡Jamás lo habría creído! ¡Jamás!"

But Henrietta dijo: "¿_Ahora_ qué piensan hacer con el asunto de comernos?"

"Por supuesto que era una broma[^broma], querida," dijo the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes. "We alligators will have our little joke, you know. Do tell your accomplished husband to come back, so that we can thank him for this fine exhibition, and then we will show you the way out of the pantano, and part in peace and goodwill."

"¡Sí, cómo no, viejo tramposo!" dijo Henrietta. "Ask him to come back so you can eat him? No, Charles will stay right where he is, in the top of that tree."

"Sus sospechas son muy injustas," dijo the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes con un suspiro. "We wouldn't harm him for worlds. We respect and admire him greatly. However, I see you are anxious to be gone, and it is indeed getting late. Mis hijos," he dijo to the other caimanes, "show these animales safely to the edge of the pantano, and see that no harm comes to them. Good-bye, my friends. I thank you all for your entertainment. Lo siento mucho que hayan tomado nuestra bromita tan en serio. However, that is past now. Sin rencores, espero."

"¡Oh, ninguno en absoluto!" dijo Henrietta sarcastically. And the Abuelo de Todos los Caimanes sank slowly out of sight.

Los caimanes mostraron a los animales a dry and easy path to the edge of the pantano, and they were very happy when they were on tierra firme once more. Charles had not come down within reach of the caimanes, but had fluttered along in the tree-tops. Then the caimanes said good-bye and wished them a pleasant viaje.

When the animales had gone on a little way, they looked back and saw the caimanes sitting in a row and looking after them, and great lágrimas were rolling from their eyes and dropping to the ground, and the sound of their sobbing could be heard for millas.

"¿Saben? Creo que de verdad les da pena que nos vayamos," dijo Alice, the pato. "I suppose it _is_ lonesome in that dreary pantano."

"¡Humph!" dijo Henrietta. "¡Claro que les da pena! But they're not crying because they like us. They're crying because they'll have to go to bed without their supper to-night."
