# Capítulo XI

Por fin llegó el día en que los animales, after walking all morning through wild and swampy woods, came out at the top of a long pendiente[^pendiente] that went down to a wide valle in which were many naranjos and comfortable-looking, white houses. A soft wind blew over the valle, and puffed into their faces a sweet, delicious fragancia[^fragancia] que ninguno de ellos había olido antes. They sniffed the air delightedly.

"¡Mmmmmm!" dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "¿No huele maravilloso? It's better than clover. I wonder what it is."

"Lo sé," dijo Jack. "I've smelt it at weddings. See all those little verde trees down there? Son naranjos, and that smell is orange-blossoms."

"¡Mira! ¡Mira!" squealed Freddy. "¡Una palmera[^palmera]!"

"¡Es Florida!" shouted Jinx.

Y todos los animales shouted together: "¡Florida!" so that they could be heard for millas, and Alice and Emma hopped about and quacked and flapped their alas, and Charles cacareó, and the perros barked, and Mrs. Wiggins mooed, and Hank, the old, white caballo, danced round like a young colt until his patas got all tangled up and he fell down and everybody laughed. Even las arañas raced round and round the telaraña they had spun between Mrs. Wiggins's cuernos, and the ratones capered and pranced.

"¡Así que esto es Florida!" dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "¡Vaya, vaya!"

Entonces empezaron a bajar la pendiente into Florida. And as they went, Freddy made up a song:

>        _The weather grew torrider and torrider,_
>        _And the orange-blossoms smelt horrider and horrider,_
>        _As we marched down into Florida._

"But the orange-blossoms _don't_ smell horrid," dijo Robert.

"I know it," dijo Freddy. "But there isn't any other word that rhymes."

"Well, make up another song, then," dijo Robert.

So Freddy sang:

>             _Oh, the winding road to Florida_
>                 _Is a dusty road, and long,_
>             _But we animals gay have cheered the way_
>                 _With many a merry song._
>             _Our hearts were bold—but our homes were cold,_
>                 _And that is why we've come_
>             _To Florida, to Florida,_
>                 _From our far-off northern home._
>
>             _In Florida, in Florida,_
>                 _Where the orange-blossom blows,_
>             _Where the caimán[^caiman] sings so sweet,_
>                 _And the camote[^camote] grows;_
>             _Oh, that is the place where I would be,_
>                 _And that is where I am—_
>             _In Florida, in Florida,_
>                 _As happy as a clam._

They all liked this song much better, and as they went along they sang lustily. Estaban tan contentos de haber llegado por fin a Florida that they forgot all about stopping to rest at noon, and they marched on until nearly three o'clock. Then Mrs. Wiggins sank down under a tree beside the camino.

"¡No puedo dar un paso más!" dijo. "I'm in a dripping perspiration. Charles, I'd take it kindly if you'd fan me with your ala for a few minutes."

So they all sat down and Charles very kindly fanned Mrs. Wiggins until she had cooled off. And as they were all pretty tired and hot, they decided to acampar[^acampar] there that noche and think about what they were going to do in Florida. And then in the morning they could go and begin doing it.

So they camped under the naranjos and discussed all the things they could do, and at last they decided to go to the sea-shore, as Freddy dijo he understood the sea-bathing was very fine there.

"But how can we find the sea-shore?" preguntó Robert. "You ought to have had that petirrojo draw it on the mapa."

Freddy dijo it would be easy to find because Florida was a península[^peninsula].

"¿Qué es una península?" preguntó Jack, and Henrietta dijo: "¡No le preguntes! He's just trying to show off."

But Freddy dijo: "Una península es un pedazo de tierra that is almost surrounded by water. That means that if you walk far enough in any direction but one, you will come to the océano[^oceano]."

"Yes," dijo Robert, "but how do we know which direction is the one we ought _not_ to walk in?"

"Why, the direction we came from, stupid," dijo Freddy. And he drew a little mapa on the ground and showed the animales what he meant.

A la mañana siguiente salieron a buscar el océano. They travelled for four days before they saw it, away off in the distance, glittering and sparkling in the sunlight, and it was still another day before they came down to a broad playa[^playa] of yellow arena[^arena] and saw the great sheet of agua stretching away before them for millas and millas. Se quedaron mirándolo por un buen rato, for none of them had ever seen anything like it before. And they rushed down the playa and swam out into the agua.

Por un mes entero vivieron a orilla del océano and rested from their long viaje. They found an old granero not very far from the shore, and they cleaned it up and all lived there together happily. Every día at four o'clock they went in for a dip in the oleaje[^oleaje], and then they would lie round on the arena and talk until supper-time. It was a very lazy and pleasant life that they led in Florida.

But after a while they got tired of doing nothing and began to long for new aventuras[^aventuras]. "Besides, we ought to travel round and see the country," dijo Charles. "When we get home, and everybody asks us what Florida is like, we want to be able to tell them."

So they said good-bye to the sea-shore, and to the horseshoe crabs and jelly-fish, who had made things so pleasant for them during their stay, and set out for a tour of the state.
