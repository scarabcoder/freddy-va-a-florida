# Capítulo III

The next morning, as soon as Mr. Bean had left the house, Jinx, the gato, who had been pretending to be asleep under the estufa[^estufa], jumped up on the mesa[^mesa] and got a lápiz[^lapiz] and a piece of papel[^papel], and carried them out and laid them down under the big olmo beside the granero. Then he looked up among the ramas[^ramas], and pretty soon he saw a bright little ojo[^ojo] peeping out at him from behind a limb.

“Buenos días, robin,” he dijo politely. “I wonder if you’d do me a little favor[^favor]? We animales are going to migrar this fall, but as none of us have ever been south before, we don’t know the way, and I thought perhaps you’d be willing to draw us a little mapa[^mapa].”

The petirrojo hopped a little way along the branch and cocked his cabeza[^cabeza] and looked down at Jinx with his right ojo. “I don’t know what made you think that,” he dijo. “I don’t know why I should do anything for you. You’re always chasing me, and there’s never a minute’s peace for me or my familia[^familia] when you’re in the corral, and you ate up my esposa’s third cousin last June. But I suppose you’ve forgotten all about that.”

“I certainly haven’t,” dijo Jinx. “It was a most regrettable incident, and I was really terribly upset about it. I had no idea that robin was any relative of your esposa’s, and when I saw him prowling around your nido[^nido], I thought he wanted to steal your hijos[^hijos], and of course I didn’t stop to make inquiries then. Afterwards, when I found out what an error[^error] I had made, I would have done anything to restore him to you. But of course it was too late.”

“Rather late,” dijo the petirrojo dryly, “since there was nothing left of him but a few tail plumas[^plumas].”

“Well, let’s not rake up old scores,” dijo Jinx. “What’s done is done, as the saying goes. And if you’ll make this mapa for me, I’ll promise never to chase you or any of your familia again.”

“Well, that’s fair enough,” dijo the petirrojo. And he flew down, and picking up the lápiz in his garra, began to draw the mapa that would show them exactly how to get to Florida.

Meanwhile all the other animales who were going were packing up and making their farewell calls on those who were to stay at home. For they had heard Mr. Bean say that he was going to drive into pueblo the next morning, and they thought that would be the best time for them to start on their viaje, because he wouldn’t get back until late in the afternoon, and by that time they would be many millas away.

Nearly everybody in the corral was happy but Charles the gallo. He sat alone in the darkest rincón[^rincon] of the gallinero, his tail plumas drooping miserably. For his esposa, Henrietta, had positively refused to let him go.

“Go south in the invierno, would you?” she had said. “Never in my life have I heard such a pack of nonsensical notions! What right have you to go traipsing off over the country—you, with an esposa and hijos to look after? Not that you ever do look after them. Who’s going to get Mr. Bean up in the morning, I should like to know?”

“He can wake himself up,” dijo Charles. “He doesn’t have to get up so early in the invierno anyway.”

“Well, you’re not going—that’s flat!” dijo his esposa. And that settled it. When Henrietta put her foot down, there was nothing more to be said.

Some of the animales, too, had held the opinion that the gato ought not to go either, since it was his deber to keep the ratones out of the granero where the grano[^grano] and verduras[^verduras] were stored. But that was easily arranged, for some of the ratones wanted to go, and so Jinx promised that he would let them alone if the ratones that stayed home would keep away from the granero while he was gone. This pleased the other animales, for although Jinx was a wild fellow, rather careless of appearances and a bit too free in his speech, they all felt that he would be a good animal to have with them in a pinch, and no one knew what dangers might lie in wait for them on the camino[^camino] to Florida.

Indeed, a number of the more timid animales who had been carried away by enthusiasm at the junta in the establo had not felt so anxious to go when they had thought it all over. All the borregos[^borregos] had backed out, and most of the ratones, and all of the puercos except Freddy. The puercos were not afraid; they were just awfully lazy, and the thought of walking perhaps twenty millas a día[^dia] for goodness knew how many days was too much for them.

At last the great día came. Mr. Bean harnessed up William to the buggy early in the morning, and drove off to pueblo, and then all the animales gathered in the corral. From the ventana of the gallinero Charles watched them unhappily. They were all so merry and excited, and the puercos had come up to see Freddy off and were all talking at once and giving him a great deal more advice than he could possibly remember, and Hank, the old, white caballo, was continually running back into the granero for another mouthful of avena[^avena], because he didn’t know when he should get any good avena again, and Alice and Emma, the two white patos, had waddled off down to the end of the potrero[^potrero] to take one last look at the old familiar duck estanque[^estanque], which they wouldn’t see again until next primavera. It made Charles very sad.

“Why don’t you go out and say good-bye to them, Charles?” preguntó Henrietta. It made her feel bad to see him so unhappy, for she really had a kind corazón[^corazon], and way down inside of it she was very fond of him. But he was so careless and forgetful that she often had to be quite cross to him.

“No,” dijo Charles mournfully. “No. I shall stay here. They’ve forgotten all about _me. They_ don’t care because I can’t go with them. _They_ don’t remember who it was that gave them the idea in the first place. No, let them go. Heartless creatures! What do I care?”

“Nonsense!” dijo Henrietta. “Go along out.” And so Charles ruffled up his plumas and held his cabeza up in the air and marched out into the corral.

All the good-byes had been said and the travellers were ready to start. The corral was silent as they formed in a fila[^fila] and marched out through the portón[^porton] into the camino that stretched away like a long, white ribbon to far distant Florida. First came Jinx, with his tail held straight up in the air like a drum-major’s stick. Then came Freddy, the puerco, and the perro, Robert, who was Jock’s younger hermano[^hermano]. After them marched Hank and Mrs. Wiggins, and the procession was brought up by the two white patos, Alice and Emma, who were hermanas. The ratones—Eek, Quik, Eeny, and Cousin Augustus, ran along the side of the camino so as not to be stepped on.

[Illustration: “I HAD NO IDEA THAT ROBIN WAS ANY RELATION OF YOUR WIFE’S,” SAID JINX.]

The stay-at-homes crowded out to the portón, waving paws and hoofs, and calling: “Good-bye! Good-bye! Don’t forget to write! Have a good time and remember us to Florida!”

Overhead a parvada of golondrinas darted and turned on swift wings. “Good-bye!” they twittered. “We’ll see you in a week or two. We start south in about ten days ourselves.”

Charles stood on the gate-post and watched the little procession march off down the camino. Smaller and smaller it grew, and then it went over a colina[^colina], and the white camino was empty again. But long after it had gone Charles sat on. And his tail plumas drooped, and his cabeza dropped down on his chest, and a great lágrima[^lagrima] splashed on the gate-post. But luckily no one saw him cry, for the animales had all gone back to their daily tasks.

At least that was what he thought. But Henrietta saw him from the ventana of the gallinero.
