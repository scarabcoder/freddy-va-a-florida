# Capítulo VIII

A la mañana siguiente, muy temprano, el señor Webb salió a tomar un poco de aire fresco antes del desayuno. Era una mañana brillante y clara. Tomó un largo sorbo de agua fresca from a raindrop, and then strolled along over the agujas de pino[^agujas], humming to himself.

>           _Oh, the winding road is long, is long,_
>             _But never too long for me. . . ._

Pronto se encontró con una hormiga[^hormiga].

"Buenos días," dijo él politely. "I am a stranger in these parts. ¿Podría decirme if there is any good fly-catching in the vecindario[^vecindario]?"

Now, almost always, if you speak to an ant, no matter how pleasantly, she will walk right by without answering. Las hormigas do this because they are always ocupadas, and they think conversation is a waste of time. But el señor Webb was a fine-looking araña, and the hormiga was rather flattered at being spoken to by him. So she dijo:

"No lo sé, señor. But there aren't many arañas in our vecindario, so I should think not."

"¡Qué lástima![^lastima]" dijo el señor Webb. "But it seems a very pleasant vecindario."

"We like it," dijo the hormiga. "Although it's not as pleasant as it used to be before the ladrones[^ladrones] hormigas came. They live in an old stump down in the woods, and they are all the time stealing our children and robbing our storehouses."

"¡Dios mío!" dijo el señor Webb. "That is very trying."

"Indeed you may say so!" respondió she. "It's hard enough to bring up a familia of fifty children without having ladrones about. Tuvimos que dejar nuestra casa and build a new one, deeper under the ground, so it wouldn't be so easy for them to break into it. Would you like to see it?"

"With great pleasure," dijo the araña; and so she led him to where there was a little hole in the ground, out of which hormigas were carrying bits of dirt and sand, which they dropped outside before hurrying back for more. She led him down the hole and into a long túnel[^tunel]. Part of the túnel was so narrow that el señor Webb had trouble squeezing through, but at last they came out in a large habitación[^habitacion] which was really the hormigas' dining-room. Here there were dozens of hormigas running to and fro, popping in and out of doorways; some of them bringing comida[^comida] which they fed to the ant children, and others carrying out dirt from the túneles and corridors they were building. All estaban demasiado ocupadas to pay any attention to their visitor, and they merely nodded and dijo: "How do," and went on with their trabajo.

"Es una casa muy agradable," dijo el señor Webb, when he had been shown through all the many habitaciones and passages.

"¡Ah, debería ver nuestra otra casa!" dijo the hormiga con un suspiro. "Gold floors in the reception hall and the dining-room, and a gold ceiling in the nursery! There wasn't a finer one in the woods."

El señor Webb pricked up his ears. "Gold floors, eh? ¿Y cómo sucedió eso?" dijo.

"Nobody knows," dijo the hormiga. "Cuando mi abuela[^abuela] first moved into the house, some of them were there, and then later, when we enlarged the house and dug out more rooms, we found more of them."

"Me gustaría ver eso," dijo el señor Webb.

"If I weren't so busy this morning, I would take you over and show them to you," dijo the hormiga.

"I'm afraid I am keeping you from your trabajo," dijo el señor Webb; "so I'll just run along. But if you will show me where your old house is, I'll run in and look at it on my way back to join my amigos."

So the hormiga went up to the door with him and showed him just which way to go, and then he thanked her politely and dijo good-bye.

Sin mucha dificultad encontró la casa that the hormigas had moved out of, and he crawled down the túnel into the empty habitaciones that had once been a happy home, but were now empty and deserted. Soon he stood in the dining-room. It wasn't very large. Even el señor Webb, who was a very small araña, could walk across it in five or six steps. But sure enough the suelo was of bright, shining, yellow oro. And there were raised letters on it, and the figure of an águila[^aguila].

Ahora bien, before he was married to la señora Webb, el señor Webb had travelled round a good deal. And once he had lived in a bank. So he knew what a twenty-dollar gold piece looks like. And now he knew that the suelo of this hormigas' dining-room was a twenty-dollar moneda[^moneda] de oro.

He did not wait to look at the other habitaciones, but hurried back to the cabaña de troncos as fast as he could go. For he remembered what the golondrina had told them about the bag of oro, and he knew that he had found the tesoro.

This was what had happened. Las hormigas que construyeron esa casa had happened to begin digging just where the bag of oro was buried. It had been buried a long, long time, and the cloth had rotted away. Las hormigas had tunnelled in and around the monedas de oro, and wherever one lay flat, they had made it the suelo or techo of a habitación.

El señor Webb regresó justo cuando los animales estaban listos para partir. They gathered round him with their orejas as close to him as they could get, so they could hear his tiny voz, and he told his story, and then they all rushed out to the ant house, and the two perros and Freddy, the puerco, started to dig. Freddy dug with his long sharp nariz, but the perros dug with their front patas. And in no time at all they had uncovered a great shining montón[^monton] of monedas de oro.

Then they were all very glad, and Charles, the gallo, was so excited that he cacareó and cacareó.

But Henrietta dijo: "¡Basta[^basta] ya con ese ruido! I don't see what you are all so happy about anyway. Now you've dug it up, what are you going to do with it?"

"Llevárselo a Mr. Bean, of course," dijo Robert.

"What are you going to do about going to Florida, then?" preguntó Henrietta. "Are you going to carry it all the way to Florida, and then back again? And what are you going to carry it in?"

"No habíamos pensado en eso," dijo the animales.

"Well, you'd better think about it now," dijo Henrietta. "The only thing you can do with the oro now is to bury it again, and get it when we come back from Florida. But I'm sure I don't know how we're ever going to take it to Mr. Bean."

"Oh, we can carry it in canastas[^canastas] or something," dijo Freddy. "Don't you worry about that, gallina."

So they scraped the earth back into the hole and covered up the tesoro, and then they started along.
