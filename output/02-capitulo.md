# Capítulo II

Now, all this time Charles had not said anything to his esposa and her ocho hermanas about what the pájaros had told him, and he had not invited them to the junta.

“She always disapproves of everything I do,” he grumbled, “and her hermanas always agree with her. It will be a much better junta if she doesn’t come. I can tell her about it afterwards.” His esposa’s nombre[^nombre] was Henrietta, and she was a very ocupada[^ocupada] gallina[^gallina], for she had ten little pollitos[^pollitos] to take care of. And so she was sometimes rather cross to Charles, who never did much trabajo and used to get in her way a good deal.

That noche[^noche], when Charles started out, she called him back and preguntó him where he was going.

“I have to attend a business junta,” he dijo importantly. “I’m expected to make a discurso[^discurso].”

“H’m, much good _your_ discurso will do anybody!” dijo Henrietta; but she was busy putting the pollitos to bed, and Charles slipped out before she had time to say any more.

The junta was a great éxito[^exito]. Nearly all the animales on the granja came, and the establo was crowded to the doors. Charles spoke long and eloquently and drew glowing pictures of what their life would be like in a southern land, lolling under the naranjos[^naranjos] and telling cuentos[^cuentos] and cracking chistes[^chistes] all day long. The puercos, who had come in a body and sat in the front row, applauded heartily, and the vacas mooed and the patos[^patos] quacked and the perros[^perros] barked, and even the ratones[^ratones], who sat in a row on one of the rafters, squeaked excitedly.

“Now, my amigos[^amigos],” dijo Charles, when he had told them all he had learned from the pájaros, “I have placed before you these facts. It remains for us to act upon them. I, for one, intend to follow the example of the pájaros and go south for the invierno. It is true that it is easier for the pájaros than it is for us. The pájaros can volar across ríos[^rios] that we shall have to swim or wade, and across montañas[^montanas] that we shall have to climb. I do not conceal from you that it may be a hard viaje[^viaje]. But it is my experience that nothing that is worth getting is easy to get. However, I shall be glad to hear what anyone else may have to say, and I accordingly throw the junta open to discussion.” And amid prolonged cheers he hopped down from the seat of the old buggy from which he had addressed the junta.

Then for quite a while the animales were much excited and all talked at once. All of them hated the thought of the long, cold invierno, and when somebody—I think it was Freddy, the smallest and cleverest of the puercos—shouted: “Why don’t we start to-night?” they all gave three cheers and started toward the puerta.

But just then Jock, the larger of the two perros, a wise old Scotch collie, got up.

“Ladies and gentlemen,” he began, “you have all heard what my amigo the gallo has said, and I think we all agree with him that it would be fine if we could all go south this invierno.” (“Yes, yes!” cried all the animales together.) “But there is one thing that I think we have forgotten. I am not a fine speaker like Charles, but I just want to say that we must not forget our deber[^deber]. We cannot all leave Mr. Bean, for he could not get along without us——”

Here Charles interrupted excitedly. “Mr. Bean!” he shouted. “What do we care for Mr. Bean? What has he ever done for us? _He_ can sleep warm these invierno nights; _he_ can have feather-beds and stoves; but _we_ don’t have such things—_we_ don’t matter! Why doesn’t he warm _our_ houses for us? Why——”

“Yes, yes, Charles,” dijo Jock quietly. “But listen to me a minute. Mr. Bean feeds us and gives us a lugar to live and looks after us when we’re enfermos[^enfermos]. We can’t just desert him, can we?”

“Well, perhaps you’re right,” dijo Charles unwillingly.

“Of course I am,” dijo Jock. And he went on to say that, while those of them whom Mr. Bean did not need during the invierno could go south if they wanted to, he thought the others should stay. “I can’t go,” he dijo. “And one of the caballos should stay to take Mr. Bean into pueblo[^pueblo] when he wants to go. And one of the vacas and some of the gallinas ought to stay, too, so he will have huevos[^huevos] and leche[^leche]. That is all I have to say.” And he bowed and sat down.

A long discussion followed, but as all the animales wanted to go, none of them except Jock would admit that they were needed on the granja. They talked louder and louder, and grew more and more enojado at each other, and it seemed likely that the junta would break up in disorder, when there was a loud ear-piercing “Meeaooouw!” and Jinx, the gato[^gato], bounded through the doorway.

In the silence that followed, all the ratones upon the rafter gave a horrified squeak, and then they rose as one mouse and tiptoed softly into a convenient hole.

“Hello, folks,” dijo Jinx breezily. “What’s all the row? I could hear you way down by the millpond, where I was hunting ranas[^ranas]. Better make less noise, or you’ll have old Bean out here with his escopeta[^escopeta]. What’s the matter anyway?”

“Fine!” he dijo when they had told him. “Fine! That’s a great idea, Charley, old boy! Didn’t think you had it in you. But see here. No use quarrelling about who’s to go and who’s not. Draw lots; that’s the way to do it. Now you say only one vaca can go. Well, here’s three of ’em—Mrs. Wiggins and Mrs. Wurzburger and Mrs. Wogus. Here, Jock, you take three popotes[^popotes] in your mouth, one long one and two short. Now let ’em draw, and the one that gets the long popote goes.”

Jock got the popotes, and the vacas drew. Mrs. Wiggins won.

“All fair and above-board, you see,” dijo Jinx. “Now, caballos next. Step up, please; it’s getting late.”

As soon as the gato had taken charge of things, the junta became more orderly, and arrangements for the departure of all those whom Mr. Bean would not need during the invierno were quickly made. Then, when everything was decided, Charles got up again to make another discurso. There wasn’t really anything left for him to say, but he was fond of making discursos, and he spoke so beautifully that everybody liked to hear him, although when they got home they could never remember anything he had said.

“Now, my amigos,” he began, “before we break up this distinguished junta, I should like to give you one thought to take home with you in your hearts—something to carry away with you as a memento of the kindness and good-fellowship we have enjoyed here together to-night. As I look about me this evening upon all these bright, eager young faces, gathered together here under one techo, it is borne in upon me——” But what it was that was borne in upon him they never knew, for at that point he stopped suddenly and climbed hastily down from the buggy seat. His esposa, Henrietta, had come in the puerta.

She marched straight down toward him between the rows of silent animales, and caught him by the ala[^ala].

“‘Bright, eager young faces,’ is it?” she exclamó angrily. “_I’ll_ give you a bright, eager young face!” And she boxed his right oreja[^oreja] with her garra[^garra]. “_I’ll_ give you something to carry home with you!” And she boxed the other oreja. “I never heard such nonsense!”

Charles hunched his head down between his hombros[^hombros]. “But, my dear!” he protested.

“Don’t you ‘my dear’ me!” she dijo. “You come along home, where you belong. Staying out all noche like this! Revelling and carousing with a lot of silly puercos and vacas that don’t know any better! The very idea!” And she pushed him unceremoniously toward the puerta.

But before they reached it another figure appeared—a short, bearded man in a long, white camisón[^camison] and carpet slippers. Mr. Bean had been awakened by the noise, and had come out to see what was the matter. He had a linterna[^linterna] in one hand and a carriage látigo[^latigo] in the other, and on his head was the white cotton gorro de dormir with the red tassel.

“You animales go to bed!” he dijo gruffly. Then he turned round and stumped back to the house.

In thirty seconds all the animales had gone and the establo was empty, except for Mrs. Wiggins and Mrs. Wurzburger and Mrs. Wogus, who lived there.
