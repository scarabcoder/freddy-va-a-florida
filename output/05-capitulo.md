# Capítulo V

Al primer destello[^destello] del día siguiente, Charles despertó[^desperto]. He stretched his alas and flapped them a couple of times, and then, before he knew what he was doing, he let out a loud cacareo[^cacareo].

He had perched on a rama of the roble, and just under him Hank was standing, fast asleep. Los caballos can sleep standing up as well as lying down, because they have four patas and don't fall over, and Hank had gone to sleep that way because the zacate[^zacate] was wet with dew, and he thought if he lay down in it, it would be bad for his reumatismo. When Charles cacareó, Hank opened his eyes.

"Goodness!" dijo. "You startled me! I thought you were not going to crow this morning. You said you were going to sleep till ten o'clock."

Charles looked a little avergonzado[^avergonzado]. "I suppose," dijo, "that I've got so in the habit of getting up early and crowing that I do it without thinking."

"Well, in that case," dijo Hank, "I don't see why you complain so much about it. If you do it without thinking, it's just like breathing, and nobody ever complains about having to breathe."

"No," dijo Charles, "that's gospel truth!"

"I expect," dijo Hank, "that you've complained about it for so long that you do that without thinking, too."

This was a little hard for Charles to understand, but he thought about it for a while. And then he dijo: "You're right, Hank. I never realized it before. I don't really mind getting up and crowing a bit, now I come to think of it. But," he added in a susurro[^susurro], "don't tell Henrietta I said so."

By the time the sol was up the animales were all up too, and getting their desayuno. Hank and Mrs. Wiggins ate the long, juicy zacate that grew beside the camino, and Freddy ate the bellotas[^bellotas] that had fallen from the roble, and Charles and Henrietta and the ratones ate hayucos[^hayucos] from a beech-tree near by. Charles and Henrietta ate the nuts whole, but the ratones held them in their little forepatas and stripped off the husks with their sharp little teeth and ate the sweet kernels. And Mrs. Wiggins gave the perro and gato some leche, and las arañas sat up between Mrs. Wiggins's cuernos, where they had spun their telaraña, and caught moscas for their desayuno.

They all had a good desayuno but Alice and Emma. Los patos like to eat the juicy weeds and things they find in the mud at the bottom of estanques, but of course there wasn't any estanque handy, so Alice and Emma ate a few hayucos that the ratones shelled for them, and dijo they would wait for the rest of their desayuno until they came to the río.

It was not until early in the afternoon that they came down a long colina into another valle and found the wide, swift río that the petirrojo had marked on the mapa. There they sat down to rest while los patos dove for their meal in the shallow agua[^agua] under the orilla[^orilla].

Mrs. Wiggins was very much interested in the diving. "I do wish I could do that," dijo. "Just think how exciting it must be to be down among the fishes and see all the queer things that grow on the bottom, and look up at the cielo through the green agua!" She had been leaning over the edge as she talked, and all of a sudden the orilla gave way, and down she went into the agua with a terrible splash, and there she was, sitting in the río with the agua up to her neck.

The animales all rushed to help pull her out, but they could do nothing for her, for she was quite helpless with laughter. She laughed and laughed. "Here I am," dijo, "down among the fishes where I wanted to be. Nothing like having your wishes come true."

But suddenly she stopped laughing. "¡Dios mío![^diosmio]" exclamó. "Where are Mr. and Mrs. Webb? They were sitting on my cabeza when I fell in."

She clambered hurriedly up the orilla, and then they all searched the bushes along the shore for a long distance río abajo. But las arañas were nowhere to be found.

"Well," dijo Mrs. Wiggins at last, "I guess they're gone. They won't drown—that's a comfort. They'll float down and land somewhere, but the corriente[^corriente] is pretty swift, and they may go many millas before they can get ashore. I don't suppose we'll ever see them again. I hope this will be a lesson to me—cutting up silly didos on the orilla like a two-weeks-old becerro[^becerro]!" She was very enojada with herself.

The animales all agreed it wasn't her fault, and pretty soon they started on again. They followed the río for some time, and by and by saw the white houses of the aldea and the high arches of the puente ahead of them.

"I vote we wait until after dark to go through the aldea," dijo Robert. "Those gente are sure to chase us or try to lock us up or something, if they see us."

This seemed a sensible plan, so they sat down by the río to wait. Pretty soon they heard a rattling and a puffing coming along the camino, and then a carro came into sight, and in it were the hombre with the bigote negro and the muchacho with the dirty cara. And behind it ran a perro negro, twice as big as Robert and three times as fierce-looking.

As soon as the hombre saw the animales he stopped the machine. "Now we've got 'em, sonny," dijo. "Here, Jack," he called to the perro. "Sick 'em, Jack! Go after 'em. Chew 'em up!"

The perro gruñó[^gruno] and bounded across the camino, but Mrs. Wiggins lowered her cuernos and shook them threateningly, and Robert barked, and the gato arched his back and spat, and even Freddy squealed enojado. And the perro stopped.

"You'd better not bother us," dijo Mrs. Wiggins.

"I don't want to," dijo the perro. "I haven't got anything against you. But he'll beat me if I don't."

"Why do you stay with him if he beats you?" preguntó Robert.

"Where could I go if I didn't stay with him?" preguntó the perro.

"Come along with us," dijo Robert, and he told him where they were going.

"That's fine!" dijo the perro, and he walked toward them, meneando[^meneando] his cola[^cola].

"Hey, Jack!" called the hombre enojado. "What's the matter with you, you useless cur? I'll beat you!" And he picked up a palo[^palo] and started after the perro.

But now that Jack had found new amigos, he wasn't afraid of his cruel amo[^amo] any more. He turned with a growl, and before the hombre could lift the palo, he was flat on his back on the camino with Jack's front patas on his chest.

Then the hombre changed his tune. "Good Jack! Good old boy!" dijo. "Let me up, that's a good perro." But Jack didn't move, and the other animales came and sat in a ring around the hombre, and the muchacho jumped out of the carro and ran away across the campos yelling, just as he had done before.

After a while, when they thought they had scared the hombre enough, they let him up, and he walked over to the carro without a word and drove off. Then Jack told them that he had lived with the hombre for five años[^anos], and that it had been a terrible life, for the hombre hardly gave him anything to eat, and beat him nearly every día.

"I guess Mr. Bean is a pretty good amo after all," dijo Hank. "At least he never beats us, and if some things aren't just as we should like them, it's because he's poor and can't afford to have them better."

"You don't happen to have a hueso[^hueso] about you, do you?" preguntó Robert. "I haven't had a good gnaw since I left home."

"The granja where I have been living is just a little way back along this camino," dijo Jack, "and I buried two good huesos in the huerto yesterday. If you'll come with me, we'll get them. Can you spare the time?"

"There's plenty of time," dijo Robert, "because we can't start on until after dark." So the two perros raced off together to get the huesos.

All this time el señor and la señora Webb had been floating peacefully down-stream on the swift corriente of the río. Las arañas can float, because they are very light. But they can't move round much on the agua, because it is so slippery under their patas, and for every step they take in one direction they slide two in another. So el señor and la señora Webb just sat still and sailed along and admired the changing scenery of the banks.

"I don't know why anyone should want a private yacht when they can travel like this," dijo el señor Webb. "It's delightful. Though I must say I am sorry to miss the viaje to Florida."

"No use crying over spilt milk," dijo his esposa. "Or spilt spiders either, and that's what we are. We'll never see those animales again. Even if we could get over to the orilla and climb up to the camino before they came along, they'd go right by without either seeing or hearing us."

"But," dijo el señor Webb, "they spoke of crossing a puente further down the río. If we got to that before they did, we could try to make them see us, anyway."

"Now, that's an idea," exclamó his esposa. "You've got a good cabeza on you, Webb. I always knew you did have, in spite of what my father dijo about you before we were married."

"I know what he dijo well enough without your repeating it every five minutes," grumbled el señor Webb. "He dijo I didn't have gumption enough to catch a lame fly without wings. That's what you're thinking about, I suppose."

"No," dijo la señora Webb, "I was thinking about the time he dijo you'd never be hanged for your beauty, and you ought to——"

"That's enough," dijo el señor Webb crossly. "You'd be better occupied thinking about what we're going to do when we get to the puente than raking up all those old things. There it is just ahead of us."

So la señora Webb stopped talking, and they began to think up a plan, and by the time they were almost to the puente, they had decided what to do.

The corriente bore them swiftly down toward one of the arches, but under the arch some dead branches were sticking up through the agua, and they caught hold of these and climbed up over them to the puente.

"They haven't come by yet," dijo el señor Webb, after they had examined all the footprints of animales plainly marked in the polvo on the floor of the puente. "There have been some caballos and perros along here to-day, but no vacas or puercos or gatos or patos."

So they both climbed up to the iron beam on one side of the puente, and each fastened the end of a hilo to the beam, and then they dropped down, spinning out the hilo as they went, and carried it across the puente and fastened the other end to the iron beam on the other side. They did this several times, until they had a bridge of hilos, strong enough to hold them both, right across the roadway about ten feet above it. Then they walked out to the middle of it and waited.

Of course they did not know that the animales had decided to wait until after dark to cross the puente, and by the time the sol had gone down and the estrellas[^estrellas] had begun to wink out, and lights to twinkle in the houses, they commenced to be worried. But there was nothing to do but wait, and at last they heard the shuffle and patter of many patas and hoofs, and the animales came down the camino and on to the puente. They were walking as quietly as possible, so that la gente in the houses would not hear them, but las arañas can see in the dark, and when Mrs. Wiggins's nariz was just under them, they each slid spinning down a hilo and landed on it.

Mrs. Wiggins gave a tremendous estornudo[^estornudo] that nearly blew them off, for they had tickled her nariz dreadfully, but they hung on tight.

"Dear me!" dijo Mrs. Wiggins. "I do hope I'm not getting a cold, being out so late in the night air!"

But el señor Webb had crawled up close to her oreja, and dijo: "It's us, Mrs. Wiggins—the Webbs. We waited for you on the puente."

Then Mrs. Wiggins told the other animales what had happened, and they were so glad that they gave a loud cheer, and they all dijo how happy they were to have the Webbs with them again, and how clever las arañas were to have thought of such a good scheme. And all the villagers came to their doors and looked out to see what the noise was, but by this time the travellers were across the puente and didn't care.

That noche they camped in a deserted granero, and it was lucky they did, for toward morning a heavy lluvia[^lluvia] came up. But the techo was still good, and though most of them woke up when the lluvia started, they were dry and warm, and soon they went back to sleep again with the pleasantest sound in the world in their orejas—the soft drumming of lluvia on shingles.
