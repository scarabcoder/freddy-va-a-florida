# Capítulo IV

And so the animales started out into the wide mundo. Although it was late in the fall and the ramas were bare, the sol was bright and the air was fresh and warm. For some time they walked along together in silence, for they were a little sad at the thought of the comfortable home and the good amigos they had left behind. But the smiling valle[^valle] through which the camino ran was too pleasant to be sad in for very long, and pretty soon Freddy, who was very clever, began to sing a canción[^cancion] he had just made up. And this is the canción he sang:

>        _Oh, the sailor may sing of his tall, swift ships,_
>            _Of sailing the deep blue sea,_
>        _But the long, white road where adventures wait_
>            _Is the better life for me._
>
>        _On the open road, when the sun goes down,_
>            _Your home is wherever you are._
>        _The sky is your roof and the earth is your bed_
>            _And you hang your hat on a star._

There were a good many other verses—too many to put down, for Freddy made them up as he went along, and there was a chorus to each verse that went like this:

>          _Oh, the winding road is long, is long,_
>              _But never too long for me._
>          _And we’ll cheer each mile with a song, a song,_
>              _So fearless and gay and free._

And pretty soon, as their spirits rose, and they thought of the adventures that lay ahead of them and the merry life they would lead, they all began to sing. They roared out the chorus with a will, and even the ratones sang in their little, squeaky voices. The ratones had got tired walking by this time, because their patas were so short, and so Mrs. Wiggins had invited them up on her back, which was so broad that there was no danger of their falling off, and they could sit there and enjoy the scenery and watch everything go by, just as you do from the ventana of a train.

All the morning they went steadily on. Every now and then they would have to go to one side of the camino to let a carro[^carro] or a farm wagon pass them, and every time that happened la gente would stare and stare. “Why, just look at those animales!” they would exclaim. “Did you ever see anything like that in your life?” And after they had gone by, la gente would stop their carros or their caballos and stare after them until they were out of sight.

About noon they climbed a steep colina, and from the top they could see ahead of them a broad valle, very much like the one through which they had come. And beyond the valle were more colinas.

“This is all strange country to me now,” dijo Hank, the old, white caballo. “I’ve driven as far as this with Mr. Bean, but I’ve never been down into that valle. We’d better have a look at the mapa.”

“There’s an arroyo[^arroyo] crossing the camino half-way down the colina,” dijo Robert, the perro. “Let’s go down there.”

So they went down and camped beside the arroyo, and the larger animales went in wading and splashed each other and laughed and shouted, and the two white patos, Alice and Emma, swam about looking like two white powder-puffs, because that is what they like to do best. But Jinx, the gato, stayed on the bank and studied the mapa that the petirrojo had drawn for him, to see if they were going in the right direction.

Then, when the animales were tired of splashing about in the arroyo, they came up on the bank and rested, and Jinx showed them the mapa. “We have to go across that valle and those colinas, and then across another valle, and more colinas, and then we come to a río,” he dijo. “And we follow the río until we come to an aldea[^aldea], and there we shall find a puente[^puente].”

“But will la gente in the aldea let us cross the puente?” preguntó Eek.

It was funny to see him and the three other ratones sitting peaceably beside the gato, but Jinx had promised not to chase them, and they were not afraid. Cats very seldom make promises, but when they do, they always keep them. Their word is as good as their bond.

“I have heard Mrs. Bean say to Mr. Bean,” Jinx respondió, “never to cross a puente until you come to it. So we’d better not worry about this one. And now don’t you think we’d better be getting on?”

So they got up and started down the colina. Half-way down they had their first adventure.

They heard a carro behind them and turned out to let it go by. It came along, rattling and bumping, for it was not a very good carro, and as it passed them, an hombre[^hombre] with a big, bigote[^bigote] negro[^negro] leaned out and stared in surprise.

“Hey, sonny,” he dijo to the muchacho[^muchacho] who was driving, “wait a minute. Look at them animales. By gum, I never see anything like that before!”

The muchacho, who had a very dirty cara, stopped the carro, and they both stared back at the animales.

“There’s nobody with ’em,” dijo the muchacho. “Who do you suppose they belong to?”

“Dunno,” dijo the hombre, and began to get out. “But we’ll just drive ’em down to my place, I guess. If they do belong to somebody, we’ll get a recompensa[^recompensa] for ’em, and if they don’t, we’ll keep ’em ourselves. The vaca looks like a good milker. Can’t say much for the caballo, though. Homely brute!”

Hank gave a loud snort at this, for while he was not a vain caballo, he had a proper pride in a neat appearance, and he thought the hombre’s remark insulting. Which indeed it was.

“That’s a nice puerco,” dijo the muchacho. “We haven’t had roast pig in a long time, pa.”

“Nor roast duck,” dijo the hombre, and he licked his bigote negro and looked greedily at Alice and Emma. “I’ll get a cuerda[^cuerda] and tie the vaca, and you take some piedras[^piedras] and drive the perro away.” He reached into the car for his cuerda.

This was too much for the animales, who had been undecided what to do.

“I don’t care for these people _at all_,” dijo Mrs. Wiggins emphatically. “Robert, you and Jinx chase that dirty-faced muchacho away before he can pick up any piedras. Don’t hurt him; just give him a good scare. I’ll attend to the hombre.” And lowering her cuernos[^cuernos] she galloped straight at him.

Now, cows are almost always good-natured and peaceful animales, and the hombre was very much surprised. He tried to dodge behind the car, but she scooped him up in her cuernos and tossed him high in the air. And as he went up, Mrs. Wiggins put her forehead to the back of the carro and pushed it ahead so that it would be under him when he came down. Which he presently did, with a thump, on the carro top. He bounced once or twice like a rubber ball; then, frightened but unhurt, peered over the edge of the top at Mrs. Wiggins, who was walking around the car and shaking her cuernos and mooing in a terribly frightening way. She was really laughing, but the hombre didn’t know that.

Meanwhile the perro and gato had chased the muchacho away across a campo. And he was even more badly frightened than the hombre; for after they stopped chasing him, he kept on running, and after he was out of sight, they could still hear his terrified yells.

“There! I guess we settled _them_!” dijo Mrs. Wiggins. And she sat down in the camino and bellowed with laughter until the tears ran down her cheeks, and the hombre with the bigote negro shivered with fear. Mrs. Wiggins was very fond of a joke.

Pretty soon the animales started on again, and when they had gone half a mile or so, they looked back and saw the hombre climb slowly down and get into the carro. But he did not come after them: he turned round and went back up the colina, and went home another way.

Mrs. Wiggins was a character. That means that when she did anything, she always did it in a little different way than anyone else would have done. And she did a good many things that nobody else would ever have thought of. There were two spiders, Mr. and Mrs. Webb, that lived up in the techo of the establo. Of course they had heard everything that had gone on the noche the animales had had their junta, and the next morning Mrs. Webb slid down a long hilo[^hilo] and landed on Mrs. Wiggins’s nariz[^nariz]. At first Mrs. Wiggins shook her cabeza and asked the araña[^arana] to get off; she tickled. But Mrs. Webb crept up close to the cow’s oreja and dijo: “I want your advice about something.”

This flattered Mrs. Wiggins, because very few people ever ask a cow’s advice about anything. So she said she would listen. Now spiders have very little voices, and even animales, who hear better than people, have to be very close to them to understand what they say. So Mrs. Webb crept still closer to Mrs. Wiggins’s oreja, and dijo: “Mrs. Wiggins, me and Webb have been talking it over, and we’d like to go on this trip with all you animales. It’s cold here in the invierno, and there are very few moscas[^moscas], and we have to sleep most of the time. Do you suppose it could be managed?”

Mrs. Wiggins thought and thought, and finally she dijo: “I’d be glad to do you and Mr. Webb a good turn, because you keep the establo clear of moscas in the verano. As far as your coming along goes, that isn’t bothering me, for you can ride on my back. But I’ve been wondering how you could catch enough moscas to keep you alive.”

“That’s just the difficulty,” dijo the araña. “We’d be travelling all día, and even if we spun a telaraña[^telarana] at noche, when we camped, the moscas wouldn’t get caught in it till next morning, and then we’d be gone.”

Mrs. Wiggins thought some more, and then she dijo: “I’ve got it! Suppose you spin a telaraña between my cuernos! Then you’ll have it with you all día, and you can catch plenty of moscas.” And Mrs. Webb was so delighted that she danced about on all her eight patas, and tickled Mrs. Wiggins’s oreja terribly, and then she ran up her hilo as fast as she could and told her esposo[^esposo]. And so they went along on the trip to Florida.

This was just the kind of thing Mrs. Wiggins was always doing.

The animales went on down the colina and across the second valle. They met a few people, in carros or on foot, but la gente only stared and did not try to stop them. Then about four o’clock Alice and Emma, who had got tired and were riding on Hank’s back, began quacking excitedly.

“There’s something funny coming down the camino after us in a nube[^nube] of polvo[^polvo]!” they dijeron.

“Carro, probably,” dijo Hank.

“It’s too small for a carro,” dijo Alice.

“Then it’s an hombre,” dijo Mrs. Wiggins.

“It’s too small for an hombre, and it comes too fast,” dijo Emma.

Then they all stopped and looked, and away back on the camino they saw a tiny nube of polvo coming along at a great rate, and they could not imagine what it could be. And then the viento blew the polvo aside for a moment and they all set up a cheer. For they saw that it was Charles, the gallo, and Henrietta, his esposa. And if you don’t believe that a gallina can run fast, you should have seen them coming down that camino.

In a very few minutes they had caught up with their amigos, and then there was a great shouting and laughing and asking of preguntas[^preguntas], but they were both so out of breath that they could not speak for quite ten minutes.

Henrietta spoke first. “Good gracious, what a día I’ve had!” she exclamó, fanning herself with her ala. “Yes, we decided to come. Charles felt so bad this morning when you all started out. So I got my hermanas to take his place in the mornings. There are eight of them, you see. That makes one for each día in the week, and one over, to look after the hijos, or help out if one of the others is enfermo.”

“But can your hermanas crow?” preguntó Freddy.

“Crow?” dijo Henrietta. “Of course they can crow! Any gallina can crow if she wants to, better than any gallo that ever was hatched.”

“Why don’t they ever do it then?” preguntó Jinx.

“Good gracious, what a silly question, gato! The gallos would never get up at all in the morning if the gallinas started to crow. They’d loaf round and sleep all día. They do little enough as it is. But at least they’re out of the gallinero early in the morning so their wives can get some trabajo done. H’m! Crow indeed! I guess not!”

The animales were all glad to have Charles and Henrietta with them, and they went on for a way, and camped that noche under a big roble[^roble] by the road-side. For a time they sat about and told cuentos and chistes and made plans for the future, but they were all tired, and one by one they dropped off to sleep. Before Charles’s ojos closed, he looked drowsily up at the starry cielo above him, and at the long, mysterious, white camino by which they had camped.

“What a wonderful time we’re going to have,” he muttered sleepily in a low voz[^voz]. “This is the first time since I was a pollito that I haven’t had to worry about getting up in the morning.

>  “_Oh, the winding road is long, is long,_
>      _But never too long for me._
>   _And we’ll cheer each mile . . . mile’th song . . . song. . . ._”

His voz trailed off into silence, and he was sound asleep.
