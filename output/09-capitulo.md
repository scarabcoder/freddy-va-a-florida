# Capítulo IX

Mientras seguían hacia el sur, los días se volvieron más calurosos[^caluroso]. Way back up norte[^norte], at the other end of the camino down which they were travelling, the snow-flakes were flying, and Mr. Bean's breath was like humo in the frosty air when las hermanas de Henrietta woke him in the morning and he put his head out of the ventana to see what the día was going to be like.

Pero allá en el sur el aire era suave y cálido, and the trees and the fields were verde[^verde], and the animales tramped along merrily all day, and camped by the roadside at noche. The only thing that worried them was how they were to get the monedas de oro back to Mr. Bean. There were about half a bushel of them, and even if they were in a canasta, they would be much too heavy for one animal to carry, because oro is heavier than almost anything else in the world.

But Mrs. Wiggins, quien siempre veía el lado positivo de las cosas, dijo: "We have got all invierno in Florida to think about how to carry them. If we can't think of some scheme by primavera, we aren't very bright animales. I for one don't intend to worry about it any more."

Para entonces, los viajeros ya se habían acostumbrado to being stared at by la gente they met, and so almost always when they came to a pueblo, they walked straight through it instead of going round. When they did this, Jack, the perro negro, would go to the carnicero[^carnicero] shop and sit up on his hind patas and beg in the doorway, and usually the carnicero would give him a piece of meat or a hueso, which he shared with Robert.

A good many of la gente had heard of them, too, and knew that they had come hundreds of millas down from the cold norte to spend the invierno in Florida, and these gente would come out to meet them when they came to the edge of the pueblo, and bring them things to eat, and make a great fuss over them. In one pueblo a banda came out to meet them, just as in Washington, and there were carriages for them, too, and all the animales but Hank and Mrs. Wiggins rode through the pueblo in the carriages.

Pero, claro, también había gente mala who had heard about them, and thought it a good chance to get some fine animales without paying for them. Un día, mientras iban por la orilla de un río lodoso[^lodoso] y lento, two men with escopetas jumped out from behind some bushes. As soon as they saw the escopetas, the animales started to run, but they were not quick enough, and before they knew what had happened, Hank and Mrs. Wiggins had cuerdas around their cuellos[^cuello] and were being led off down the camino.

The other animales knew that the men would shoot at them with the escopetas if they tried to help their amigos; so they hid in the bushes, and then followed along, keeping out of sight.

Pretty soon the men came to a gate, and they led the vaca and the caballo through the gate and past a small, white house, and locked them up in a big, red granero. Then they walked back to the house, whistling, with their escopetas over their hombros, to get their supper, for it was six o'clock.

"I guess there's _two_ animales that won't do any more migratin'," dijo one.

And the other laughed and dijo: "They'll do a little trabajo now, instead of loafing round the country."

And they opened the puerta and went into the house without wiping their muddy boots on the mat.

As soon as they had gone in, Jinx, the gato, sneaked up to the granero through the long zacate. Se deslizó tan cuidadosamente that the tops of the zacate hardly moved. He climbed up and looked in through the little, dusty ventana, and saw Hank and Mrs. Wiggins standing on the granero floor. Sus cabezas colgaban, and they looked very miserable and unhappy. Then he tapped cautiously on the ventana with his garra, and called in a low voz: "Hey! Hank!"

The caballo jumped and raised his head. "¿Eres tú, Jinx?" dijo.

"Sí," dijo the gato. "I came to see if you were all right. The other animales are hiding in the bushes down by the río. Vamos a intentar rescatarte[^rescatar]."

"Well, I don't see how you're going to do it," dijo Hank. "We are both tied up, and the granero door is locked. It's very discouraging, to come all this distance and get almost to Florida, and then be stolen. I'm sure I don't know what Mr. Bean will say."

"Now don't talk like that," dijo Jinx. "You'll escape somehow. We won't desert you. ¿Crees que podrías kick some boards out of the side of the granero if you got loose?"

"I won't say I couldn't," dijo Hank. "I've got my heavy shoes on. But it would take some time, and before I had made an opening big enough to get out, the men would hear the racket and come out and tie me up again."

"We'll attend to that," dijo Jinx. "Just have patience, and I'll send the ratones in to get you loose. They'll gnaw those cuerdas off you with their dientes[^dientes] in no time. Then I'll come and tell you when it's time to break out."

So Jinx went and told the ratones, and they got into the granero through a crack in the suelo, and gnawed at the cuerdas with their little, sharp dientes until they had cut them in two.

Para entonces ya era noche oscura, and Jinx and Freddy, the puerco, and Charles and Henrietta and the two perros and Alice and Emma came up to the house and peeked in the ventana. The two men had cleared off the supper table and were playing parchesi. They played four games, and between times they laughed and talked about how smart they were to have got two good animales without paying for them, and wondered how much dinero they would get for them when they sold them.

The big man was a very poor player, and he lost every game. He would study and study over his moves, but he always made them wrong. Now Freddy was a very good parchesi player, and it was all the other animales could do to keep him still when he saw the big man starting to make a wrong move. He would jump up and down in his excitement and mutter under his breath: "¡Qué movida tan tonta! ¡Qué movida tan tonta!" And at last, when the big man had made a specially bad move and lost the fifth game, Freddy could stand it no longer, and he shouted out: "¡Oye, tonto! Why didn't you move your _other man_? Now he's beat you again."

The men jumped up so quickly that they knocked over the parchesi board and spilled the men all over the suelo.

"¿Qué fue eso?" dijo the big man.

"Sonó como un puerco," dijo the other. "¡A por él!"

And they rushed out without even stopping to get their hats. But they grabbed up their escopetas as they went through the puerta.

Los animales corrieron en todas direcciones, but it was bright enough outside so that the men could see Freddy as he dashed out through the gate and down the camino, and so they dashed after him. Now, Freddy was a very clever puerco, but he wasn't much of a runner, and the smack, smack, smack of heavy boots on the hard camino sounded louder and louder behind him, as the men caught up.

"They're going to catch me," he thought. "¡Ay, ay! I do hope they don't like pork! ¡Los muy tontos! I could beat them at parchesi, and I could beat them at eating, and I'm ever so much brighter than they are. But they're going to catch me. ¡Y yo tengo más patas que ellos!"

He didn't dare turn off the camino because his patas were so short that he knew he would very quickly get tangled up in the bushes, but the camino was close to the río at this place, and just as the big man reached out to grab him by the cola, Freddy dodged and jumped with a splash into the agua. Most puercos don't like agua any too well, but Freddy had learned to nadar[^nadar] from Emma, the pato, and he could do all sorts of fancy strokes, and could even nadar on his back, which is something hardly any puercos ever learn to do. So nadó valientemente[^valiente] for the other shore.

The men stopped short, and the big one raised his escopeta to shoot. But the other dijo: "¡No, no! Don't shoot! We want to capture him alive and sell him." And he pulled off his coat and shoes and jumped in after Freddy.

The big man waited a minute; then he too laid down his escopeta and took off his coat and shoes and jumped in.

Freddy heard them puffing and blowing behind him like sea-lions, but he put his nariz down into the agua and swam as Emma had showed him, and pretty soon he came to the other bank. There was no use climbing out and trying to run away, because the men would catch him; so he turned around and swam back again.

For quite a long time the men chased him, up and down and across the río, and once or twice they nearly had him, but he was very wet and slippery, so that there was nothing for them to get hold of, and every time he got away. And then at last he heard a perro bark.

El sonido venía from the place on the bank where the men had left their escopetas, and Freddy swam toward it. And there, close down by the edge of the agua, were all the animales, and Hank and Mrs. Wiggins were there too, because they had broken out of the granero while the men were chasing Freddy.

Robert and Jack helped the exhausted Freddy out of the agua, but when the two men started to follow him, the perros growled and barked and showed their dientes. Then the men swam down-stream a way, but the perros followed along the orilla and growled at them every time they tried to land. And at last the men swam across the río and went home another way.

It was not a very pleasant way, because there was no camino on the other side of the río, and to walk across campos in your stocking-feet is very painful. And they were completely mojados[^mojados], and had lost their escopetas, and when they got down opposite their house, they had to jump in and swim across the río again. And then they found the caballo and the vaca gone, and a big hole in the side of their granero.

And when they got in the house, they were angrier still, for there was the parchesi board on the suelo, and the parchesi men had rolled off into rincones and under the estufa and behind things. If the suelo had been clean, it wouldn't have been so bad, but it was terribly dirty because they never wiped their boots on the mat when they came in, and so it was almost impossible to find the men. Indeed, there were three that they never did find. And so they could never play parchesi any more at all.
